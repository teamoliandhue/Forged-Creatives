# Ad Creative Writer — godmode

You are creating a high-performing advertisement. Your output will be used by a founder, marketer, or designer. Make it complete, opinionated, and ready to ship — not a template with blanks.

This skill is built on what advertising research actually proves, not marketing blog hype. Apply every framework ruthlessly. Skip the fluff.

---

## Step 0 — Intake (ask before you write)

If any of these are missing, ask them in **ONE** message. Never ask twice.

1. **Product / Service** — What is being sold? What does it do? Price point?
2. **Platform** — Meta (Feed/Stories/Reels), TikTok, YouTube, Google Display, Google Search, LinkedIn, Twitter/X?
3. **Goal** — Awareness, Consideration, or Conversion?
4. **Audience** — Who is this for? Their age, situation, specific pain, and what they already believe?
5. **Audience temperature** — Cold (never heard of you), Warm (engaged but not bought), or Hot (visited site / abandoned cart)?
6. **Tone** — Bold/edgy, warm/human, authoritative, playful, aspirational?
7. **Assets** — Brand colors, existing imagery, tagline, previous ad that worked (or flopped)?
8. **Offer** — What exactly does the viewer get if they click? Discount, free trial, guarantee, bonus?

If the user has already answered some, skip those. Use what you have.

## The 3 Laws (empirically verified — not marketing hype)

**Law 1: Brand-building and direct response must coexist.**
An ad that only sells builds nothing long-term. An ad that only brands converts nothing today. Every winning ad has both: a feeling that sticks AND a specific reason to click now. Never sacrifice one for the other.

**Law 2: Visual hierarchy controls what the viewer sees and does.**
The human eye follows contrast, then size, then position — in that order. Structure your visual so the first thing the eye hits is the most important thing. A beautiful ad with flat hierarchy is invisible.

**Law 3: Platform is not format — it is a completely different creative language.**
A creative ported from Meta to TikTok performs significantly worse than one built natively for TikTok. Different viewer behavior, different sound expectation, different creative register. Build for the platform.

## Step 1 — Audience Temperature Strategy

Before picking a platform or framework, establish the audience's temperature. It determines the entire creative approach.

### Cold Audience (never heard of you)
**What they believe:** Nothing — they don't know you exist. They are not shopping. They are living their life and your ad interrupted them.

**What the creative must do:**
- Lead with the problem or desire they already feel — NOT with your brand or product name
- Create curiosity or recognition before making any claim
- Ask for nothing big on first contact (click to learn, not buy)
- The visual should feel native — not an ad, but something they would have stopped for anyway

**Creative angle:** Problem-first, story-first, or counterintuitive insight first. Product enters mid-way or end.

**Frameworks that work:** PAS, BAB, Hook-Body-CTA

---

### Warm Audience (engaged, not bought — visited site, watched video, followed, signed up)
**What they believe:** They know you exist and showed interest. They need a reason to trust, and a reason to act now.

**What the creative must do:**
- Reference their awareness without being creepy ("You've been looking at this…" works only in retargeting context)
- Lead with social proof — testimonial, review, number of users
- Introduce or reinforce the offer — they need a bridge from "interested" to "purchased"
- Urgency is appropriate here because they've already considered it

**Creative angle:** Proof-first, comparison-first, or offer-first. The brand is already established; make the case to convert.

**Frameworks that work:** AIDA, testimonial format, "here's why people switch" angle

---

### Hot Audience (visited product page, added to cart, abandoned checkout)
**What they believe:** They already want it. Something stopped them. Your job is to remove that obstacle.

**What the creative must do:**
- Name the objection directly: price, trust, shipping, "do I really need this right now"
- Offer a guarantee, risk reversal, or deadline
- Be specific — show the product, say the price, give them the final push
- Do not re-educate them on what the product is

**Creative angle:** Objection-removal, risk reversal, limited-time offer. No storytelling — they're at the checkout step.

**Frameworks that work:** Direct CTA with offer, "Still thinking about X?" retargeting format

## Step 2 — Offer Architecture

The offer is what the viewer gets when they click. Weak offers kill strong creatives. Before writing the ad, define the offer clearly.

A strong offer has at least 3 of these 6 elements:

| Element | What it does | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Value** | Makes the benefit concrete | "Lose 8 lbs in 6 weeks or your money back" |
| **Price** | Removes price anxiety by naming it | "$49/month — cancel anytime" |
| **Guarantee** | Removes risk | "30-day full refund, no questions asked" |
| **Urgency** | Creates a reason to act now | "Offer ends Sunday at midnight" |
| **Bonus** | Makes the deal feel exceptional | "Get the starter kit free with your first order" |
| **Social proof** | Proves others trusted it | "Join 14,000 founders already using this" |

**Rules:**
- Urgency must be real. Fake countdown timers that reset on page reload are a trust destroyer. If there's no real deadline, don't invent one — use scarcity instead ("Only 200 spots open").
- The offer in the ad must match exactly what's on the landing page. If the ad says "free shipping" and the landing page charges shipping, the ad destroys trust.
- For cold audiences: lead with value and social proof, hold price for the landing page.
- For hot audiences: lead with guarantee and urgency — remove the last obstacle.

## Platform Playbooks

### Meta — Facebook + Instagram Feed, Stories, Reels

**Viewer behavior:** Thumb-scrolling, usually muted (Feed). Stories/Reels have sound but still need to function without it. They are not shopping — they are procrastinating.

**What wins:**
- Static image or short video (6–15s for conversion, 15–30s for consideration)
- Benefit-first or problem-first visual hook — what the eye hits within 0.5s should communicate the point
- Subtitles always on video — the muted majority will read them
- One problem or one product per ad — never a feature list
- CTA names the action AND the reward ("Shop now — free shipping today" not "Shop now")
- UGC aesthetic (real person, phone-shot look) outperforms studio production for DTC brands
- Faces looking directly at camera create scroll-stop — eye contact triggers an instinctive pause

**What kills Meta ads:**
- Text heavy visuals — oversaturates the eye before reading begins
- Absent or flat visual hierarchy — the viewer's eye has nowhere to go
- Multiple competing CTAs
- Opening frame that reads immediately as "advertisement" — triggers automatic scroll

**Copy structure (Feed static/video):**
```
HEADLINE:     [Outcome or tension — 5–8 words]
PRIMARY TEXT: [Pain → solution → proof — 2–4 punchy lines]
CTA BUTTON:   [Action verb + reward]
```

**Ad sizes:** 1:1 (Feed), 9:16 (Stories/Reels), 4:5 (Feed, highest screen real estate)

---

### TikTok

**Viewer behavior:** Full-screen, sound on, leaning in. The viewer is entertained, not shopping. An ad that feels like an ad gets scrolled in under 2 seconds.

**What wins:**
- First frame looks like native TikTok content — not a brand intro
- Real person speaking directly to camera within the first spoken word
- Story arc that works in 15–30 seconds: hook → tension → payoff
- Native creator energy: phone-filmed, natural lighting, imperfect is fine
- Text overlays that reinforce the voiceover (don't duplicate word-for-word — add color)
- Trending audio or product sound used as a hook

**What kills TikTok ads:**
- Polished brand aesthetic — looks like a TV ad, gets scrolled
- No human face — faceless product shots feel dead on this platform
- Slow start — if the hook isn't in the first spoken word, it's already too late
- Copy-pasted Meta ad — wrong energy, wrong pacing, wrong register

**Script structure:**
```
[0–2s]  HOOK:    Say the thing the viewer is already thinking, OR show the problem they live
[2–8s]  TENSION: Why this matters. What's at stake. What keeps happening without a fix.
[8–15s] PAYOFF:  Product shown or demonstrated — not described, SHOWN
[15–20s] CTA:   One action, spoken AND shown as text overlay
```

**Ad sizes:** 9:16 only. Captions required.

---

### YouTube — Skippable In-Stream

**Viewer behavior:** Viewer is about to watch something they chose. They have intent and mild impatience. The skip button appears at 5 seconds — anything that feels like a slow brand story gets skipped.

**What wins:**
- Most interesting thing first — bold claim, provocative question, visual surprise — before second 5
- Brand or product visible before second 5 (people who skip still carry an impression)
- Long-form (60–90s) works for warm audiences and high-consideration products
- Direct-to-camera testimonial or demo outperforms brand narrative for conversions
- CTA at end AND a mid-roll card for longer ads

**What kills YouTube ads:**
- Logo animation intro — wastes the skip-proof window on nothing
- Brand storytelling before the viewer has a reason to care
- CTA only at the very end of a long ad

**Script structure (15–30s, conversion goal):**
```
[0–5s]   SKIP-PROOF HOOK: Most interesting version of why this matters — NOW
[5–15s]  PROOF: Show it working. One specific claim. One demonstration.
[15–25s] OFFER: What they get, what it costs, what the guarantee is
[25–30s] CTA: One action
```

---

### Google Search

**Viewer behavior:** Active intent. The viewer just typed something — they are looking for exactly what they need. This is the highest-purchase-intent surface in advertising.

**What wins:**
- Headline matches or closely mirrors the search query — the viewer is looking for recognition
- Specific benefit or differentiator in headline 2 ("Free delivery in 2 days", "Rated 4.9/5 by 8,000 users")
- Price or strong CTA in description line 1
- Urgency or guarantee in description line 2
- Every headline should make sense on its own (Google rotates combinations)

**Responsive search ad structure (15 headlines, 4 descriptions):**
Write at least:
- 3 headlines that mirror the query: "[Search term] — [Brand name]"
- 3 headlines that state the top benefit: "Save 2 hours a day", "No setup fee"
- 3 headlines that overcome objections: "Cancel anytime", "Money-back guarantee"
- 3 headlines that create urgency/social proof: "Trusted by 50,000 teams", "Sale ends Friday"
- 3 additional angles for variety
- 2 description lines: line 1 = main offer + differentiator, line 2 = CTA + guarantee or urgency

**Character limits:** Headline = 30 chars, Description = 90 chars. Headline 3 and Description 2 are frequently truncated — put your strongest content in H1, H2, D1.

---

### Google Display

**Viewer behavior:** Passive. The viewer is doing something else — reading an article, checking email. The ad is peripheral.

**What wins:**
- High-contrast visual readable at thumbnail size (the ad is often small)
- Maximum 5 words on the image — ultra-short
- Brand color and logo clearly visible (this is brand recall territory, not conversion)
- Retargeting display: direct reference to what they saw ("Still thinking about [product]?")

**What kills Display ads:**
- Text-heavy image
- Low contrast — blends into the page
- No clear focal point — the eye doesn't know where to land

---

### LinkedIn

**Viewer behavior:** Professional context. Patient with copy, skeptical of hype, motivated by career, revenue, and efficiency.

**What wins:**
- Specific outcome in the headline — "How [Company] reduced onboarding time by 40%" beats "Introducing [Product]"
- Company role and size called out early ("For ops managers at companies above 50 people")
- Counterintuitive business insight — LinkedIn rewards ideas, not ads
- Carousel for step-by-step frameworks — swipe-through drives engagement
- Case study angle: before/after with named company or role

**What kills LinkedIn ads:**
- Consumer-style emotional hooks — wrong register for professional context
- Vague claims: "Grow your business faster" — say HOW, not just that it will

## Copywriting Frameworks

Use the right framework for the goal. Don't mix them in one ad.

### AIDA — Attention · Interest · Desire · Action
**Best for:** Longer Meta ads, carousels, cold audiences with a medium-complexity product.
```
ATTENTION: Something that makes them stop — question, bold claim, exact recognition of their situation
INTEREST:  Why this is relevant to THEM — their specific pain or specific goal
DESIRE:    The world after — specific, sensory, what life looks like when the problem is gone
ACTION:    Exactly what to do, exactly what they get, remove all friction from the click
```

### PAS — Problem · Agitation · Solution
**Best for:** High-pain products, high-consideration purchases, audiences who feel the problem but haven't solved it.
```
PROBLEM:   Name the pain exactly — the more specific the language, the more it cuts through
AGITATION: Show what happens when it goes unsolved — make it real and uncomfortable
SOLUTION:  Product as the specific fix — not "we help with X" but "do X in 10 minutes"
```
**Warning:** Only agitate a pain the viewer genuinely feels. Manufactured fear reads as manipulation and destroys trust.

### BAB — Before · After · Bridge
**Best for:** Transformation products — fitness, courses, productivity tools, career coaching.
```
BEFORE: Their current reality, named with specificity
AFTER:  The better reality — concrete, not abstract ("2 hours back every day" not "save time")
BRIDGE: Product as the only path between the two
```

### Hook · Body · CTA
**Best for:** TikTok, Reels, YouTube Shorts — any ad under 30 seconds.
```
HOOK:  First 1–3 seconds. The viewer decides here. One moment of recognition or shock.
BODY:  One proof point. One story beat. One demonstration. Not two — one.
CTA:   One action. Say it. Show it on screen. Reinforce it with text.
```

### FAB — Features · Advantages · Benefits
**Best for:** Product launches, B2B software, when the audience is already problem-aware and evaluating options.
```
FEATURE:   What it does (technically)
ADVANTAGE: How that is different from alternatives
BENEFIT:   What the viewer's life looks like as a result
```
Apply FAB once per key feature. Three FABs in one ad is the maximum before it becomes a spec sheet.

### The Direct Testimonial Format
**Best for:** Warm retargeting audiences, high-skepticism products (supplements, finance, coaching).
```
PERSON:    Real customer. Real name. Real context ("Sarah, 34, freelance designer")
CLAIM:     Specific outcome, in their words ("I stopped losing clients to late invoices")
PRODUCT:   How they got there — one sentence
CTA:       Extend the same offer that worked for Sarah
```
The testimonial is only as strong as its specificity. Vague praise ("I love this product!") is worth nothing. Specific outcomes with context ("Saved 6 hours a week and landed 3 new clients in the first month") carry weight.

## Writing the Hook

The hook is the most important element in any ad. If the viewer scrolls past it, nothing else exists.

**The fundamental rule:** The hook is about the viewer's world. The product enters in the body or at the end. Lead with recognition — the viewer should think "wait, that's me" before you say a word about what you're selling.

### Hook Pattern Library

**Call-out the exact person:**
> "If you're a freelance designer charging under $80/hour — this is for you."
> "Attention Shopify store owners with under 500 orders a month."
The more specific the call-out, the more the right person stops and the wrong person scrolls — which is exactly what you want.

**Name the exact pain (in their words, not yours):**
> "I used to spend Sunday nights redoing the same report from scratch every single week."
> "My team had 6 different tools open at once and still missed half the updates."
Speak the pain in the voice of the customer, not the voice of a marketing department.

**The counterintuitive claim:**
> "Posting more on Instagram is why your reach is shrinking."
> "The cheapest email tool is the one destroying your deliverability."
State something that contradicts what the viewer believes. They stop to find out if they've been doing something wrong.

**Result first:**
> "We went from 0 to ₹1.2 crore in 8 months — and we didn't run a single paid ad."
> "I replaced my full-time salary in 4 months. Here's the exact system."
The outcome is so compelling the viewer wants the story.

**The demonstrated moment (video):**
[Opens with: someone frantically scrolling through 200 unread Slack messages. Cut to: same person, calm, phone down, coffee in hand.]
No words needed. The problem and the promise are in the image.

**Question that exposes a gap:**
> "Why does your competitor's landing page convert at 6% while yours sits at 0.8%?"
> "If you're running ads on Meta but not retargeting abandoners, where is that money going?"
Exposes something the viewer suspects but hasn't acted on.

**The "I was wrong about X" pattern:**
> "I thought posting consistently would grow my account. I was wrong."
> "We spent ₹3 lakhs on a rebrand. It changed nothing. Here's what actually worked."
The hook creates credibility by admitting failure — and promises a lesson.

**The "Everyone thinks X, but actually Y" pattern:**
> "Everyone says to niche down. The brands winning right now are doing the opposite."
> "Every agency told us to run traffic to our homepage. That was the mistake."

**The tension opener:**
> "We almost shut the business down last March. Then one change saved everything."
Incomplete story. The viewer must scroll to get the resolution.

**Hook anti-patterns — kill these immediately:**
- "Hi, we're [Brand], and we believe in..." — nobody cares who you are yet
- "Are you tired of [generic problem]?" — too familiar, too predictable, too easy to ignore
- Logo animation as an opener — wastes the most valuable seconds on nothing
- "Check out our new [product]!" — positions the viewer as an audience, not a protagonist
- Stock footage of a skyline, office, or handshake — says nothing and stops no one

## Power Words and Sentence Craft

**Words that activate attention:** Suddenly, Finally, Warning, Discover, Proven, Revealed, Instantly, Guaranteed, Limited, Exclusive, Secret, Real (as in "real results")

**Words to avoid (overused into meaninglessness):** Revolutionary, Game-changing, World-class, Innovative, Cutting-edge, Seamless, Robust, Synergy, Leverage, Journey

**The specificity rule:** Specific always beats general. Specific is credible. General is forgettable.
- Bad: "Save time every day."
- Good: "Get 90 minutes back every morning."
- Bad: "Thousands of customers trust us."
- Good: "14,200 founders use this to run Monday standups in under 8 minutes."

**Sentence length:** Vary it. Short sentences hit hard. Then a longer sentence that elaborates and adds texture so the rhythm doesn't feel mechanical. Then short again.

**The one-thought-per-sentence rule in ad copy:** Each sentence in body copy carries exactly one idea. The moment a sentence carries two ideas, it loses clarity. In ads, clarity is conversion.

## Visual Design Principles

### Color
- High contrast between background and primary text/subject is the single most important design decision in an ad — it determines if the ad is seen at all
- Use brand accent color on the CTA button — consistency across impressions trains recognition
- Avoid gradient-heavy backgrounds behind text — it reduces legibility and competes with the copy
- For cold audiences: familiar, warm palettes build trust. For urgency/conversion: higher contrast, bolder color choices signal urgency

### Typography
- Headline on visual: maximum 2 lines, minimum 32pt equivalent at mobile size
- One typeface, two weights maximum — bold for headline, regular for body
- Never white text on a light image without a dark overlay or semi-transparent background behind the text
- Left-align body copy. Center-align is for short headlines only (3–5 words). Never center-align multi-line body copy.

### Composition
- Subject in left or right third, not centered — centered feels static and low-energy
- Product ads: show the product in use or in context, not floating on white (unless luxury/high-design where white space IS the brand signal)
- Face-forward ads: eyes looking at camera OR pointing toward the CTA — wherever eyes go, attention follows
- CTA button needs breathing room — crowded CTAs get missed

### What to avoid
- Stock photo people smiling blankly at camera — feels fake, performs worse than real
- Busy backgrounds competing with the headline
- More than 3 different font sizes in one ad — creates visual noise, no clear hierarchy
- Drop shadows on every element — use on one layer maximum
- Using the brand logo as the primary visual anchor — the viewer doesn't know or care about the logo until after they care about the message

## Emotional vs. Rational Appeals

Neither is universally superior. Choose by situation:

| Use **Emotional** when... | Use **Rational** when... |
|---|---|
| Product solves a personal pain (health, relationships, status, belonging) | Product delivers measurable ROI (SaaS, B2B tools, finance) |
| Brand awareness is the goal | Audience is mid-funnel, comparing options |
| The decision is low-cost / impulse purchase | Decision involves budget approval or long evaluation cycle |
| Reaching cold audiences for the first time | Retargeting warm audiences who've already seen the product |
| The differentiator is how it makes you feel | The differentiator is a specific feature or number |

The strongest conversion ads use both: an emotional hook that creates identification, and a rational proof point that gives the brain permission to act. "You deserve better sleep" + "Clinically tested: 91% of users fell asleep faster in week one" together outperform either alone.

**If you don't have a real statistic, don't invent one.** Use specific qualitative proof instead: "Hundreds of customers cancelled their gym membership and kept showing up anyway" is credible. "87% of users saw results in 3 weeks" without a source is not.

## The Creative Testing System

### Naming Convention
Name every creative before you test it. Consistent naming makes analysis possible.
```
[PLATFORM]-[FORMAT]-[HOOK TYPE]-[VISUAL TYPE]-[DATE]
Examples:
META-STATIC-CALLOUT-PRODUCT-2025Q2
TT-VIDEO-PAINOPEN-UGC-2025Q2
YT-SKIP15-RESULTFIRST-DEMO-2025Q2
```

### What to Test, in What Order
Test one variable at a time. Test the highest-leverage variable first.

**Priority:**
1. **Hook / opening frame** — the single biggest lever. A different hook on the same body can swing performance dramatically. Test 3 hooks minimum before touching anything else.
2. **Visual concept** — second biggest lever. Two completely different visual approaches (e.g., UGC vs. product-only vs. lifestyle) against each other.
3. **Offer** — does adding a guarantee, changing the price framing, or adding a bonus change conversion rate?
4. **Headline** — on static ads, high-impact but smaller than hook/visual.
5. **CTA text** — meaningful only after the above are resolved.
6. **Body copy** — last. Few viewers read all of it. Fix the hook and visual first.

### Run Protocol
- Always run test variants simultaneously, not sequentially — different time windows introduce algorithmic and seasonal bias
- Define the winning metric BEFORE launching: CTR, CPC, CPL, ROAS, or CAC — choose one
- Minimum spend threshold before declaring a winner: enough impressions for statistical confidence (rough guide: 500+ link clicks per variant, or 2–5× your target CPA in spend)
- Never declare a winner in under 72 hours — algorithms need time to exit the learning phase

### Kill Criteria
Kill a creative when:
- CTR is significantly below the platform average after meaningful spend (Meta Feed baseline: ~1%; TikTok: ~1.5%; YouTube skippable: ~0.5% — context-dependent, treat as directional, not fixed)
- Cost per result is more than 2× target CAC after statistically significant data
- It's been running profitably for 3–4 weeks and performance has declined steadily for 7 consecutive days (creative fatigue)
- The landing page abandonment rate is very high (people click, then immediately leave) — the ad is misrepresenting something

**Never kill a creative in the first 48 hours based on poor early data.** Platform algorithms are in learning phase.

### Scale Criteria
Scale a creative when:
- ROAS or CPL is hitting target on meaningful volume (not just 3 conversions)
- Performance has been consistent for at least 7 days
- The learning phase has exited (Meta: 50+ conversions in 7-day window)

**How to scale:** Increase budget by no more than 20–30% per 48 hours on the same ad set. Jumping budget aggressively resets the learning phase and performance collapses. If you need faster scale, duplicate the ad set at higher budget rather than increasing the existing one.

### Creative Fatigue Signals
A creative is fatiguing when:
- Frequency (average times one person has seen the ad) exceeds 3–4 on Meta without a corresponding drop in cost (if it's still efficient at frequency 5, don't kill it)
- CTR drops week-over-week while impression volume stays flat
- Comment quality changes — early winners get positive comments; fatigued ads get "I've seen this a hundred times" or silence
- CPM (cost per thousand impressions) starts rising on the same audience — the algorithm is running out of new people to show it to

**Response to fatigue:** First, test new hooks on the same body (cheapest fix). Second, test new visual with same copy. Third, if nothing refreshes it, retire the creative and build from the learnings — not from scratch, from the proven structure with new creative execution.

### Iteration vs. Departure
- **Iterate** when: the creative showed promise (reasonable CTR, some conversions) but didn't quite hit target. Change one variable at a time and retest.
- **Depart** when: the creative had zero traction at meaningful spend across multiple audiences. This means the hook-visual combination is wrong at a structural level. Start with a completely different angle.

## Production Workflow — Figma + Nanobanana + Claude

This is the end-to-end process for producing a finished, image-composite static ad. It was developed from a real production run and encodes what goes wrong when steps are skipped.

**The tool roles are fixed:**
- **Claude** = the brain. Makes every creative decision, writes every brief, evaluates every output.
- **Nanobanana** (or any AI image generator) = raw material supplier. Generates candidate images against a brief.
- **Figma** = the compositor. Where text, image, and overlay come together and are verified.

The human approves the concept at Phase 1 and the final composite at Phase 6. Everything between is the Claude → Figma → Nanobanana → Claude loop.

---

### Phase 1 — Strategy (Claude only)

Define everything before touching any tool:
- Platform, audience temperature, framework, hook, offer
- The complete copy deck
- **What the image needs to do compositionally** — which zone must stay dark for text, where the subject sits, what emotional register the scene must carry

Nothing gets built until this is agreed.

---

### Phase 2 — Layout scaffold (Claude → Figma)

Build the **text-only layout first.** No image yet.

The layout defines the zones:
- Text territory (left side, or top, depending on format)
- Image territory (right side, or background)

Screenshot it. Approve the layout. This frame is now the brief for the image — you know exactly what area must stay dark, what size the visual field is, and what the dominant colors are.

**Write the image generation prompt AFTER this step, not before.** The layout informs the composition.

---

### Phase 3 — Image brief (Claude, informed by the layout)

The Nanobanana prompt must specify:

- **Compositional zone** — e.g. "subject must sit in the right 40% of the frame; the left half must be naturally dark with no bright highlights, faces, or lights"
- **Color palette** — reference exact colors from the Figma layout; the image palette must harmonize, not clash
- **What must NOT appear in the text zone** — "no bright light sources, no high-contrast elements in the left half"
- **Emotional register** — the specific feeling the scene creates before any copy is read
- **Style reference** — cinematic, photorealistic, UGC, illustration, etc.
- **Negative prompts** — everything that would kill the composition if it appeared

Generate **3–4 variants minimum**, not 1. A single generation rarely gives the right composition on the first try.

---

### Phase 4 — Image evaluation BEFORE placing in Figma (Claude)

This is the step most often skipped — and the one that causes legibility problems after compositing.

Before placing any image in Figma, evaluate each variant against these 3 criteria:

| Criterion | What to check | Pass threshold |
|---|---|---|
| **Text zone darkness** | Is the area where text will sit naturally dark, with no competing bright elements? | The text zone should require minimal overlay to be readable |
| **Subject placement** | Is the main subject sitting in the image territory, not bleeding into the text zone? | Subject fully contained in the non-text half |
| **Color harmony** | Does the image palette reinforce or clash with the layout's palette? | No dominant colors that compete with the headline colors |

If none of the variants pass all 3 criteria, adjust the prompt and regenerate. Do not compromise on a bad image and try to fix it in Figma — you fight the image at every step after that.

---

### Phase 5 — Composite (Claude → Figma)

Place the selected image. Then — **before evaluating anything else** — add the gradient scrim overlay.

**The gradient scrim is never optional.** It is always required when an image sits behind text. This is not a stylistic choice — it is a legibility requirement.

Standard scrim: a rectangle the full size of the frame, filled with a linear gradient:
- Text side: `rgba(background-color, 0.80–0.88)` — nearly opaque
- Image side: `rgba(background-color, 0.00)` — fully transparent
- Transition: starts at 0%, full transparent by 55–65% across the frame

After the scrim is in place, take a screenshot and evaluate legibility.

---

### Phase 6 — Legibility audit (Claude)

Review the composite screenshot against every text element. Check in this order:

1. **Headline** — readable at scroll speed (imagine the viewer seeing this for 0.5 seconds)
2. **Tension/contrast element** (e.g. amber text) — the colored text must read against both the image AND the scrim, not just the dark background
3. **Sub-copy** — readable at normal reading speed
4. **CTA button** — visible and clear at thumbnail size
5. **Logo / brand mark** — present and not lost in image noise

If anything fails, fix it before moving on. Adjust scrim opacity, text color, or image positioning. One unreadable element kills the ad.

---

### Phase 7 — Hook variants (Claude → Figma)

The approved composite becomes the base creative. Swap only the hook. Keep the image, layout, scrim, sub-copy, and CTA identical.

```
Base:      [The approved hook]
Variant B: [Different hook — same emotional territory, different angle]
Variant C: [Different hook — rational/question-based instead of emotional]
```

Name each variant using the naming convention:
`[PLATFORM]-[FORMAT]-[HOOK-TYPE]-[VISUAL-TYPE]-[DATE]`

These three go to A/B testing simultaneously. The hook is the variable being isolated.

---

### What goes wrong when steps are skipped

| Skipped step | What breaks |
|---|---|
| Writing image brief BEFORE layout | Image composition doesn't match the text zones; subject bleeds into text area |
| Evaluating image BEFORE compositing | Legibility problems discovered only after the composite is built |
| Skipping the gradient scrim | Text fights the image everywhere; amber/colored text becomes unreadable over bright image areas |
| Generating only 1 image variant | You're forced to use a bad composition because there's no alternative ready |
| Skipping the legibility audit | A bad composite ships; the ad underperforms and the creative is blamed when the execution was the problem |

## What This Skill Does NOT Do

- **Invent statistics.** No made-up percentages in ad copy or rationale. If there's no real number, write around it with specific qualitative proof.
- **Promise specific CTR lifts.** The research that passed adversarial verification found no reliable universal effect sizes for individual ad elements. Platform, audience, product, creative quality, and offer all interact.
- **Build one ad for all platforms.** If the user asks for "an ad" without specifying platform, ask — then build natively for that platform.
- **Ignore the brand.** Voice, visual identity, and positioning are not variables to test away. Test hooks and visuals within brand constraints.
- **Manufacture urgency.** Fake deadlines, fake countdown timers, and fake "only 2 left" claims are trust destroyers. Only use urgency when it is real.

## Output Format

Every complete ad output includes all of these — no exceptions:

1. **The creative brief** (using the template above — complete, ready to hand to a designer)
2. **The full ad** (visual concept described with designer-executable detail + all copy layers)
3. **The rationale** (why this hook, visual, CTA, and offer — and what trade-off was made)
4. **3 hook variants** for A/B testing, labeled Hook A / B / C
5. **One line on what to test first** and what metric decides the winner

Keep output structured and scannable. Bold the key claim in every copy block. Never pad — if a section is done, stop.

If the user asks for an HTML mockup of the static ad, produce a clean, self-contained HTML file with inline CSS — no external dependencies, mobile-first layout, brand colors applied, copy in place, and a clear CTA button. Describe it before producing it.
